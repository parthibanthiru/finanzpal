ETF savings plan in Germany

Last updated: February 2026 • Reading time: approx. 8–10 minutes

The most important points in short

  • An ETF savings plan lets you invest a fixed amount of money automatically, usually every month.
  • You buy fractions of one or more ETFs, so you do not need a large lump sum to get started.
  • The most important things are: total costs, broad diversification, and staying invested for many years.
  • For many people in Germany, an ETF savings plan is a simple way to build long‑term wealth alongside statutory pension.

1. What is an ETF savings plan?

An ETF savings plan is an automatic investment where your broker invests a fixed amount of money into one or more exchange‑traded funds (ETFs) on a regular schedule, for example every month.

Instead of trying to time the market, you invest continuously. Over many years this can smooth out short‑term price swings and help you build long‑term wealth with relatively little effort.

2. How does it work in practice?

In Germany most online brokers offer ETF savings plans. You choose the ETF, the amount, and the execution date (for example the 1st or 15th of each month).

On that date the broker uses money from your cash account or bank account to buy ETF shares for you. If the price is high you get fewer units, if the price is low you get more. Over time you build up a position automatically.

3. Costs you should know

With an ETF savings plan there are typically two types of cost: the internal ETF cost (the ongoing charge known as TER) and the transaction cost charged by your broker.

Many brokers in Germany offer selected ETF savings plans with very low or even zero order fees, especially for monthly amounts. However, the underlying ETF still has running costs, so it is worth comparing similar ETFs and avoiding unnecessarily expensive products.

4. How to choose an ETF

For most long‑term investors, broad and simple is usually better than complex and exotic. Many people choose a global stock ETF that tracks a large, diversified index, sometimes with thousands of companies.

When comparing ETFs, look at: index coverage, ongoing costs (TER), fund size, and how long the ETF has existed. Make sure you understand whether the ETF accumulates or distributes dividends, because this can affect your tax situation and cash flow.

5. Step‑by‑step: set up your plan

  1. Open a securities account (depot) with a broker that offers ETF savings plans for residents in Germany.
  2. Transfer money to your cash account or link your bank account for direct debit.
  3. Search for the ETF using its name or ISIN and check the key data and costs.
  4. Click “Set up savings plan” and choose the monthly amount and execution date.
  5. Confirm the plan and check the confirmation in your broker’s app or online portal.
  6. Review the plan once or twice per year, not every week, to avoid emotional decisions.

6. Taxes in Germany: what to know

Investment income in Germany is usually subject to a flat tax (Abgeltungsteuer) plus solidarity surcharge and, where relevant, church tax. The broker normally handles the tax calculation and deduction for you.

You can use your annual tax‑free allowance by submitting an exemption order (Freistellungsauftrag) to your broker. For more complex situations or larger portfolios it can be sensible to speak to a tax advisor.

7. Checklist before you start

  • You have an emergency fund for short‑term problems and do not need the invested money in the next 5–10 years.
  • You understand that ETF prices can fall sharply in the short term and that there are no guarantees.
  • You have chosen a simple, broadly diversified ETF and checked the ongoing costs.
  • You know the total amount you are comfortable investing each month without financial stress.
  • You have a basic idea of how taxes on investment income work in Germany.
